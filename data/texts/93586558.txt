     The average household in the United States contains over one-hundred thousand unique items. These items range from single-use items such as toothpicks, paper plates, and toilet paper to vintage family heirlooms. Many of the items we use every day are ultimately destined for increasingly crowded landfills or to become pollution in the natural environment. People complain of having too much clutter, yet each day we are bombarded by advertising that is carefully (and expensively) designed to make us want to buy and have more.

      We are constantly led to believe that we will be happier with the next acquisition- the next outfit, the new computer, the iced-coffee from the drive-thru. Indeed, when we buy, we typically get a surge of dopamine that is delightful, but short-lived. One consequence of this is that we are left perpetually wanting something more, and feeling as though we are worth less if we have less. Assigning value our lives based on the quality and quantity of our material possessions greatly dimishes quality of life for everyone involved, as we become more focused on things than experiences and relationships. Of course, in the quest for more things, we spend more of our time working in order to have money to buy them, losing our lives in pursuit of purchasing power. Meanwhile, as consumers work to buy and manufacturers work to produce, retail executives profit massively while often paying as little as possible to laborers. This creates an exploitative system that compromises the stability of our entire society. The materialism of modern society has a negative effect that permeates our entire culture.
